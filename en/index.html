Blog

Key points to know when choosing and using a hearing aid.

Signs of hearing loss

Turning up the TV volume, frequently saying “pardon?”, difficulty in noisy places...

Read more
  • Difficulty distinguishing speech in crowded environments.
  • Missing words during phone calls.
  • Needing higher TV/phone volume than people around you.
  • Fatigue: Increased “listening effort” by the end of the day.

If you notice these signs, the best next step is to confirm with a hearing assessment and choose the right solution.

Behind-the-ear vs in-the-ear: pros / cons

Selection is based on aesthetics, power needs, ease of use and ear anatomy together.

Read more
Behind-the-ear (BTE/RIC) — generally more powerful and flexible
  • Pro: Wider power range; adaptable to different hearing losses.
  • Pro: Controls can be more accessible; practical to use.
  • Pro: Easy parts replacement (tube/receiver/dome).
  • Con: The housing is visible behind the ear; may be felt with glasses/masks.
In-the-ear (ITE/ITC/CIC/IIC) — more compact options
  • Pro: Less visible; may suit users with high aesthetic expectations.
  • Pro: Mics closer to the natural ear position can improve directional cues.
  • Con: More exposed to earwax/moisture; may require more frequent filter changes.
  • Con: Power may be limited for very severe losses (depending on model).
Note: The best choice is made with audiological results + ear anatomy + daily needs.

First adaptation period

The brain “re-learns”. Gradual use and regular check-ups deliver the best results.

Read more
  • Week 1: Start at home and with short outdoor trials.
  • Weeks 2–4: Increase time in busy environments; personalize with fine-tuning.
  • Follow-up: A few quick visits in the first month can significantly improve comfort.

Early use: speech discrimination & perception support

Early, well-fitted hearing aid use may help speech understanding and adaptation.

Read more
Speech Discrimination / Speech Understanding
  • SD test: Shows how accurately you identify words/speech (often reported as a percentage).
  • Early intervention: If auditory input stays low for a long time, listening effort can increase; early support may ease adaptation.
  • Goal: Hear speech cues more clearly and accelerate the brain’s re-learning process.
Perception in daily life
  • First days: Some sounds (paper rustling, dishes) may feel prominent; this is often normal.
  • Gradual use: Increasing usage time gradually and regular fine-tuning improves performance.
  • Follow-up: Fitting and dome/mold selection affect both SD and comfort.
Note: SD results vary by loss type/severity and test conditions.

Care: filters & cleaning

Regular care preserves performance and reduces the risk of malfunctions.

Read more
  • Moisture/dust: Store in a dry, clean place; consider a drying kit.
  • Filters: Earwax buildup can reduce sound; periodic replacement is recommended.
  • Troubleshooting: If sound is lower, first check the dome/filter/cleaning.

Bluetooth & connectivity

Your phone model, OS and device family determine the connectivity experience.

Read more
  • Streaming: Listen to calls, music and video audio through the hearing aid (compatibility dependent).
  • Hands-free: In some combinations, handle calls without touching the phone (model dependent).
  • LE Audio / Auracast: Next-gen broadcast/streaming for supported devices (model dependent).

Frequently Asked Questions

Audiological measurements (audiogram), degree of hearing loss, and lifestyle needs are evaluated together. Ear anatomy, ease of use and connectivity expectations also affect the final choice.

The brain “re-learns” new sounds. Environmental sounds may feel different at first. With gradual use and regular fine-tuning, most users adjust noticeably within a few weeks.

The goal is not only to increase volume; it is to improve speech clarity by managing noise and prioritizing speech. That is why proper fitting is critical.

There is no single “best.” Behind-the-ear models often offer wider power ranges and easier handling. In-the-ear models may provide aesthetic advantages. The decision depends on hearing loss degree, ear canal anatomy and daily needs.

Rechargeable models are convenient. Battery-operated models can offer uninterrupted use by swapping batteries and may be more flexible for some users.

Many models support Bluetooth connectivity; however, compatibility depends on your phone model, operating system and the device’s connectivity standard. We check compatibility to determine the best pairing.

Feedback is often caused by dome/mold fit, earwax, loose placement or incorrect settings. With proper fit and feedback control settings, it is usually resolved quickly.

Daily surface cleaning and protecting from moisture improve performance. For in-the-ear models, filters/guards may need periodic replacement depending on usage and earwax.

More frequent visits are recommended during the initial period. After that, a 6–12 month interval is usually sufficient for periodic check-ups.

Warranty terms vary by model and supply channel. After diagnosis, repair or part replacement is planned accordingly.
